Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD+) is a coenzyme found in every cell. It participates in hundreds of metabolic reactions—most notably those involved in mitochondrial energy production and DNA repair.

## Why levels decline

NAD+ levels decrease with age in humans and animal models. The mechanisms are still being mapped, but research points to increased consumption (particularly by CD38 and PARPs) and reduced salvage pathway efficiency—not simply "making less."

This decline matters because NAD+ is required for sirtuin activity, PARP-mediated DNA repair, and mitochondrial function. The relationship between NAD+ pools and aging phenotypes is an active research area—not a settled narrative.

## Precursors: NMN and NR

Two dietary precursors have been studied in human supplementation trials: nicotinamide mononucleotide (NMN) and nicotinamide riboside (NR). Both can raise blood levels of NAD+ metabolites in published studies.

They are not identical. Conversion pathways differ. Individual response varies. This is why we disclose doses of both in Cellular Energy rather than picking one based on marketing trends.

## Methylation matters

High-dose NAD+ precursor supplementation increases demand on methylation pathways. Trimethylglycine (TMG) donates methyl groups and supports homocysteine metabolism—this is why Cellular Energy includes TMG alongside NMN and NR.
