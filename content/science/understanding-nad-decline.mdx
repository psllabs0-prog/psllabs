Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD+) is a coenzyme found in every cell. It participates in hundreds of metabolic reactions—most notably those involved in mitochondrial energy production and DNA repair.

## Why levels decline

NAD+ levels decrease with age in humans and animal models. The mechanisms are still being mapped, but research points to increased enzymatic demand (particularly by CD38 and PARPs) and reduced salvage pathway efficiency—not simply reduced biosynthesis.

This decline matters because NAD+ is required for sirtuin activity, PARP-mediated DNA repair, and mitochondrial function. The relationship between NAD+ pools and aging phenotypes is an active research area—not a settled narrative.

## Precursors: NMN and NR

Two NAD+ precursors have been studied in published research trials: nicotinamide mononucleotide (NMN) and nicotinamide riboside (NR). Both can raise blood levels of NAD+ metabolites in published studies.

They are not identical. Conversion pathways differ. Response varies across models. This is why Cellular Energy discloses doses of both for reference—not as a recommendation for personal use.

## Methylation in the research literature

High-dose NAD+ precursor models show increased demand on methylation pathways. Trimethylglycine (TMG) donates methyl groups and is referenced in homocysteine metabolism studies—this is why Cellular Energy documents TMG alongside NMN and NR.
