Senescent cells are cells that have stopped dividing but remain metabolically active—often secreting inflammatory signals collectively called the senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP). Clearing or modulating these cells is a target in longevity research.

## Senolytics defined

Senolytics are compounds studied for their ability to selectively affect senescent cells. Fisetin, a flavonoid found in strawberries and other plants, has been classified as a senolytic in preclinical research—including studies showing effects on senescent cell burden and healthspan markers in aged mice.

## The evidence gap

Most senolytic research is preclinical. Human trials are emerging but limited. This is not a reason to dismiss the mechanism—it is a reason to be precise about what we know and what we do not.

The Foundation compound profile includes fisetin at 100 mg—a dose range referenced in published animal studies. We cite the research. We do not promise outcomes.

## How fisetin fits the profile

Foundation documents fisetin alongside trans-resveratrol (sirtuin signaling), spermidine (autophagy support), and methylated B vitamins (methylation cofactors). Four actives, four disclosed doses—for research reference and documentation, not as a prescribed formula.

## Reading the literature

When evaluating senolytic claims, ask: Was the study in cells, animals, or humans? What was the dose? Was it isolated fisetin or a blend that hides amounts? PSL's approach is disclosure first, mechanism second, marketing never.
