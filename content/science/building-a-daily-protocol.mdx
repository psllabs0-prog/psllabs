Research supply catalogs often add complexity—multi-ingredient blends, undisclosed doses, and marketing-first positioning. The analytical literature points in a different direction: a small number of well-characterized compounds, documented at disclosed amounts, with batch-matched testing records.

## Start with documentation

Before integrating any material into a workflow, review the lot-specific Certificate of Analysis. Confirm identity, purity, and contaminant panels meet your requirements. One well-documented compound tells you more than five with incomplete records.

## Group by mechanism, not marketing

Compound profiles can be organized by research area—base signaling pathways, NAD+ metabolism, mitochondrial function—without implying a prescribed personal protocol. Each profile should add distinct, documented mechanisms—not redundant versions of the same active.

## Consistency in record-keeping

Laboratory workflows depend on reproducible documentation. Lot numbers, COA references, and storage conditions should be tracked consistently—not evaluated on subjective timelines.

## Evaluate on evidence, not promises

Cellular and molecular research operates on long timescales. If a supplier promises rapid outcomes without citations, ask for the underlying data. PSL publishes references and batch records so you can evaluate materials on evidence.

## Audit before adding

When reviewing new materials, compare against what is already in your inventory. Overlapping actives, duplicate precursors, or redundant documentation add cost and confusion without adding clarity.
