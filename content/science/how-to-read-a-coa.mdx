A Certificate of Analysis (COA) is a lab report for a specific batch of material. It is not marketing. It is not a summary brochure. It is the document that tells you whether what is in the container matches what is on the label.

## Why COAs matter

Many suppliers do not publish full COAs. They publish "tested for purity" badges without naming the lab, the methods, or the actual results. A real COA names all three.

## What to look for

**Lot number.** Every COA should reference the exact batch it tested. Match this to the lot number on your label.

**Testing lab.** Look for an independent, accredited laboratory—not "in-house testing" or an unnamed facility. ISO 17025 accreditation is the standard reference for analytical labs.

**Identity testing.** Confirms the compound is what the label claims. Usually performed by HPLC (high-performance liquid chromatography) against a reference standard.

**Potency.** Confirms the amount of active ingredient matches label claim within an established tolerance—not "present" but at a specific concentration.

**Contaminants.** Heavy metals (lead, arsenic, cadmium, mercury) and microbial limits. These panels matter as much as potency.

## Red flags

- COAs without lot numbers
- Undisclosed blend products with no per-ingredient results
- Summaries that don't list methods or raw data
- Labs you cannot verify exist
